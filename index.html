Desert Yard Notes for Phoenix & the Valley

What actually survives in Maricopa County dirt, and how to water it.

Phoenix is not a hard place to grow things; it is a hard place to grow the wrong things. The Valley sits in USDA zones 9b to 10a on old alluvial soil that is alkaline, low in organic matter, and frequently underlain by caliche — a cemented calcium layer that stops shovels, roots, and drainage cold. Add 110-plus-degree summers and roughly eight inches of annual rain and the rulebook from wetter climates simply does not apply.

Two lawns a year, or none

The Valley's turf tradition is really two lawns in rotation: Bermuda through the hot months, then an optional overseed of perennial ryegrass for winter color when Bermuda goes dormant. Overseeding is a choice, not a requirement — skipping it saves water and lets the Bermuda come back stronger in spring. Mid-October is the classic overseed window, once nights drop below about 65°F.

Caliche and what to do about it

If a posthole fills with water and holds it overnight, you have hit caliche. Planting holes need to punch through it or drain around it, or trees slowly drown in a bathtub of their own irrigation. This single subsurface fact explains a large share of the Valley's dead citrus and stunted mesquites.

Irrigation is the whole game

Summer turf here needs deep, infrequent soaks — wetting the soil 8 to 10 inches down, then letting it dry — while desert-adapted trees prefer long, slow drip cycles out at the canopy edge, not against the trunk. The region's water agencies publish excellent free guidance; the Water – Use It Wisely program (born in Arizona) keeps a practical landscape watering guide tuned to Valley seasons.

The desert-adapted shortlist

  • Trees: desert willow, palo verde, ironwood, Chinese pistache for shade with fall color.
  • Shrubs: Texas sage, brittlebush, little-leaf cordia.
  • Accents: agave, red yucca, desert spoon — sculptural, nearly waterless.
  • Groundcover: decomposed granite beats gravel for looks and heat re-radiation; skip plastic sheeting under it.

Timing notes

Plant desert trees and shrubs in fall, not spring, so roots establish before the furnace arrives. Prune frost-tender plants only after the last freeze risk (late February most years). Fertilize Bermuda from May to August, never in winter dormancy.
